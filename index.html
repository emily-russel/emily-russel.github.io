Failing The Poor

The U.S. tax code is the biggest anti-poverty program we have -- and it's not working.

The Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) is lauded as a strong anti-poverty program that both encourages and rewards work. According to research by the Center on Budget and Policy Priorities, in 2018, the EITC “lifted about 5.6 million people out of poverty, including about 3 million children.” The program also enjoys bipartisan support, particularly because the program rewards work. In 2014, Paul Ryan, then Chairman of the House Budget Committee, supported expanding the program for childless workers, saying the EITC “is one of the few programs that have shown results. It encourages people to work by increasing the rewards of work.” Most recently, President Joe Biden’s administration also proposed a measure to expand the program for working childess adults, although this was ultimately not included in the American Rescue Plan Act of 2021. In 2020, approximately 25 million taxpayers received EITC benefits.

Millions of Americans count on receiving support from the EITC. But, as Matt Bruenig of the People’s Policy Policy Project points out the program -- if it is truly intended to fight poverty -- is flawed in some irredeemable ways. By requiring participants to work, the program simply excludes the poorest families and children from receiving any support. Bruenig appropriately asks: “What kind of anti-poverty program helps the poorest less, not more?” Additionally, because tax preparation filing can be a cumbersome and complicated process, only 78% of eligible taxpayers even receive EITC benefits, dramatically reducing the impact of the program. Finally, for those who do claim the credit, many taxpayers are using paid tax preparation services that effectively take a cut of the benefit.

How does the EITC work?

The EITC is a federal tax credit for low and moderate income working people. The credit is refundable, which means that even those who owe no tax can receive its benefits in the form of a tax refund. The amount of the credit depends on earned income, marital status, and number of qualifying children. The credit matches dollars of earned income -- up to a certain point. As shown in the chart below, the EITC is a "trapezoid program." The credit phases in by matching each dollar of earnings with additional credit. For example, a single parent with one child would receive $0.34 for each dollar they earned in the 2014 tax year. At a certain point, the benefit reaches a maximum (in this case, $3,305). The credit remains at $3,305 until our example parent earns $1 over $17,830. This extra dollar, and each dollar afterward, reduces the credit by about $0.16 until the credit eventually reaches zero.

The EITC is considered an important way to combat child poverty, thus the credit is much lower for taxpayers with no qualifying children. However, the credit design also excludes families with no earned income (including those receiving unemployment or Social Security). It also excludes many non-citizens permanently living in the United States who would otherwise be eligible. In effect, the poorest children and families do not receive this benefit at all.

The EITC is further devalued by the paid tax preparation industry.

Approximately 60% of EITC recipients spend a portion of their credit on tax preparation fees. A 2016 report from the Progressive Policy Institute found "zip codes with the highest concentrations of EITC filers have approximately 75 percent more tax preparation storefronts per filer than moderate EITC zip codes, and large chain preparers tend to cluster in high-EITC zip codes." The report also estimates that EITC recipients using paid services spend an average of $400 at national preparation chains. Some tax companies even offer "early refunds" and "advance loans" on tax refunds -- another opportunity for companies to profit off the poor.

But it shouldn't be this way.

All EITC filers are eligible to file their federal taxes for free through the IRS Free File Program. The Free File program is a public-private partnership intended to provide free online tax preparation and filing to most taxpayers. The Free File program is available to all but the top 30% of taxpayers. For example, in 2014, Free File was available to tax filers with an Adjusted Gross Income (AGI) of $58,000 or less.

The following Tableau visualizations use data derived from the Tax Policy Center's Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) Interactive Database, which has information on 2014 tax returns with an AGI of $60,000 or less. This means that most, but not all, filers in this dataset were eligible for Free File.

High rates of EITC claims are correlated with higher rates of filers using paid preparation.

Most eligible taxpayers do not use the Free File Program.

In 2019, ProPublica reported on TurboTax’s 20-Year Fight to Stop Americans From Filing Their Taxes for Free: "The centerpiece of Intuit’s anti-encroachment strategy has been the Free File program, hatched 17 years ago in a moment of crisis for the company. Under the terms of an agreement with the federal government, Intuit and other commercial tax prep companies promised to provide free online filing to tens of millions of lower-income taxpayers. In exchange, the IRS pledged not to create a government-run system." ProPublica found that Intuit, the parent company of TurboTax, deliberately hid access to the free filing pages from Google and other search engines. Moreover, the software has its own version of a "free" product that promises a free return but is rife with traps that force filers to pay.

In 2020, the Treasury Inspector General for Tax Administration audited the Free File program in response to concerns raised by Congress about whether the program is operating as intended. In short, the program is not operating as intended.

Eligible taxpayers aren't using the program.

In 2019, only 2.4% of the 104 million eligible taxpayers obtained a free return through the program.

Many taxpayers are being redirected to paid products.

More than 34.5 million taxpayers (of the 104 million who met Free File criteria) used a commercial software to file their tax return.

14 million taxpayers paid unnecessary fees.

An estimated 14 million taxpayers (of the 104 million who met Free File criteria) may have paid a fee to electronically file their federal tax return in the 2019 filing season.

The tax code is failing the poor.

The EITC is deeply flawed. On the anti-poverty front, the design intentionally excludes millions of Americans who are too poor to receive the benefits. It is difficult to claim, perhaps unintentionally, without the help of corporate tax preparers. As a work incentive, the program is on “morally questionable ground” at best. At worst, it might not work at all! Flawed as it may be, the EITC remains a vitally important source of income for many Americans. This fact, paradoxically, has created a reflexive, bipartisan consensus that the EITC is the best way to alleviate poverty. It is not. Given the stakes, we must be prepared to radically change how we provide cash assistance to those who need it most.
